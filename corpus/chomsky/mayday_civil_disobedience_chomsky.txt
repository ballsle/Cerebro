Mayday: The Case for Civil Disobedience
By Noam Chomsky
The New York Review of Books, June 17, 1971

Columnist Mary McGrory observed that the May Day demonstrations received harsh press coverage, yet she recognized their actual purpose. The demonstrators sought to connect public opposition to the war itself, using their presence as a form of pressure rather than seeking media approval.

Nicholas von Hoffman's reporting captured the demonstrators' character more accurately. He noted that thousands of ordinary people — those with exams, jobs, and families — had risked arrest for their convictions. Their actions transformed Washington into a militarized zone, demonstrating serious commitment to ending the war.

The April-May Washington demonstrations began with Vietnam veterans' guerrilla theater and escalated into massive civil disobedience. Hundreds of thousands demonstrated at the Capitol; veterans testified at Congressional hearings; thousands engaged in passive resistance at government offices. Over 15,000 participated in Mayday actions, many submitting to repeated arrest and harsh treatment. Supporting demonstrations occurred nationwide.

The Nixon Administration's "plan" for Indochina involved gradual withdrawal of ground troops while maintaining technological warfare and expanded reliance on Vietnamese forces. However, ordnance expenditure escalated dramatically under Nixon, with bombing reaching 130,000 tons in March 1969 and remaining high as of March 1971.

The air war expanded into Laos and Cambodia, with evidence indicating rural populations as primary targets. The war's human cost remained staggering. Senator Kennedy estimated 25,000-35,000 Vietnamese civilian deaths annually. Herbert Mitgang cited evidence of an additional half-million civilian casualties and three million refugees generated in recent years.

Daniel Ellsberg and other informed observers concluded that Nixon would not terminate aerial warfare without being forced. Confidential briefings suggested five to ten years of continued warfare, with potential 50,000 American troops indefinitely stationed in Vietnam.

The Administration appeared to be adopting a classical colonial warfare pattern, relying more on mercenary and native forces while maintaining air and helicopter superiority. American conscripts had become viewed as a mistake; professional warriors or remote technicians were better suited for colonial conflict where civilian populations constituted the enemy.

The strategy required "cooling America" — convincing the population to tolerate indefinite destruction. The Nixon-Kissinger calculation assumed Americans would permit the war to continue for perhaps fifteen to twenty years. The Administration believed military efforts must continue to "give valued time" since Washington assessed that "South Vietnam is simply not ready in many respects to try to cope with Communist political challenges."

Regarding the strategic importance of maintaining "the cooling of America," several constituencies required pacification for the plan to succeed. Congress, despite recent talk, had taken little substantive action. The administration must also repress and discredit groups maintaining dramatic focus on war urgency and threatening to disrupt American society's orderly functioning.

During the Constitutional Convention, Edmund Randolph warned (as reported by James Madison) that executive power, especially during wartime, required regular punishment mechanisms: "Should no regular punishment be provided, it will be irregularly inflicted by tumults and insurrections."

Given continuing American aggression, was civil disobedience justified? Without demonstrations, draft resistance, and antiwar actions, "the cooling of America" would have succeeded, leaving few executive constraints with unimaginable consequences. Many converted hawks testified that demonstrations significantly influenced their evolution.

Some argued civil disobedience was undemocratic or displayed "totalitarianism of the left." This argument suggested that democratically controlled institutions determining war continuation meant citizens must obey, refraining from illegal disruption of the war machine destroying helpless people. By this logic, democratic principle required Indochinese suffering resulting from American institutional failure.

The harder questions are: Is the disobedient actor right? Will the action achieve just ends? Would strictly legal means prove ineffective? How do law obedience's overall social consequences compare with disobedience's? Are nonparticipants injured or unfairly inconvenienced?

Approximately 15,000 people, mostly young, attempted disrupting federal government functioning on May 3, mainly through marching or blocking traffic. The government response was instructive. Passive demonstrator groups were forcibly dispersed. Police essentially refused arrest, foreclosing passive civil disobedience options at designated points.

Police dispersal and arbitrary mass arrest tactics, though unsurprising, were clearly unlawful. Washington Post assistant news editor Henry Allen was illegally arrested, spending twenty-one hours in custody. Howard Zinn was arrested May 4 asking a policeman why he was beating a long-haired young man merely walking, with no demonstration nearby.

Henry Allen noted being impressed, while jailed, by "the tough, almost amused cynicism of people who are no longer surprised that other Americans will sweep them off the streets on charges so ridiculous that no one even bothered to laugh at them."

Much more ominously, this reaction reflected growing contempt for American society's institutions, contempt inspired by hypocrisy, lies, and brute force from the Administration, apparently determined to demonstrate — trivially in Washington and vastly in Indochina — that it regards law as instrumental to its purposes rather than as principle.

Suppose the President continues military victory pursuit and Congress fails acting. Then war-ending seekers can accept defeat, submit to police terror through passive disobedience, or escalate confrontation. The state nearly monopolizes violence means, and state violence and elective despotism support likely mounts as confrontation escalates. However, the Administration's criminal policies and Congress's weakness and complicity may leave no alternatives for those remaining seriously committed to halting Indochina's murder and destruction.

Is it unimaginable that substantial populations, shown the Nixon-Kissinger calculation's error, would march on Washington prepared for civil disobedience if war continues? Through its decision to block protest channels and continuing criminal war commitment, the government may provoke domestic crisis of indeterminable scale.
