What Makes Mainstream Media Mainstream
Noam Chomsky
Z Magazine, October 1997

I write about the media because studying it reveals patterns in intellectual culture. Media output occurs daily, allowing systematic investigation of what receives emphasis and how information gets structured.

The media operate similarly to scholarship and opinion journals, though with additional constraints. Understanding any institution requires examining three elements: internal structure, broader social positioning, and documented records from leaders within that system about their actual operations.

Media analysis involves developing hypotheses based on institutional structure, then investigating whether actual media products conform to those predictions. Most research focuses on carefully studying media output against reasonable assumptions about how these institutions function.

Two Media Sectors

Mass media targets general audiences through entertainment and local newspapers. Elite media—including the New York Times and CBS—sets agendas for everyone else. These outlets reach privileged audiences: wealthy people and the "political class" involved in managing systems—politicians, executives, academics, and journalists.

Elite media establish frameworks that other outlets follow. As "Notice to Editors" announcements demonstrate, smaller papers often follow agenda-setting outlets because they lack independent research resources. Breaking from this pattern invites pushback; editors face economic and professional consequences for sustained deviation.

Structural Analysis

The real mass media primarily divert public attention toward entertainment rather than serious matters. Meanwhile, elite media corporations are "very profitable" and often owned by larger conglomerates like General Electric and Westinghouse—organizations at the top of private economic hierarchies.

These institutions interact with government, other corporations, and universities. Since media function as doctrinal systems, reporters consulting expert sources at universities and think tanks like Brookings receive frameworks for interpreting events.

Universities themselves aren't independent institutions. They depend on "private wealth, big corporations with grants, and the government." People internally who reject these structures face filtering throughout their education, from kindergarten through elite colleges like Harvard and Princeton.

The Orwell Example

George Orwell's suppressed introduction to Animal Farm addressed "Literary Censorship in England." Though Britain lacks secret police like the Soviet Union, independent thinkers get excluded nonetheless. Orwell identified two mechanisms: press ownership by wealthy interests controlling what reaches the public, and elite education teaching which thoughts are "proper to say."

The Product Model

The New York Times, analyzed as a corporation, sells audiences—not newspapers. The product targets privileged decision-makers, while advertisers (other businesses) purchase access to these audiences. Predicting media output follows logically: content will reflect interests of buyers and sellers, institutional power structures, and surrounding systems.

Testing this prediction yields strong support. This hypothesis holds better than most social science conclusions, which makes sense given the structural forces involved.

Taboo Topic

Discussing these dynamics remains completely forbidden in academic settings. Kennedy School, Stanford, and similar institutions rarely permit examination of whether media serve elite interests. This suppression itself follows logically from institutional analysis—those in power naturally resist exposure.

This isn't purposeful censorship but rather that candidates reaching such positions have already internalized acceptable frameworks. The left and right alike apply this screening.

Internal Doctrinal Beliefs

Examining how elites discuss these matters among themselves reveals underlying philosophy. Public relations figures, public intellectuals, and academic political scientists consistently describe ordinary citizens as "ignorant and meddlesome outsiders" who must remain "spectators, not participants."

The general public votes occasionally to "pick out one of us smart guys," then leaves decisions to "responsible men"—defined as those obedient to power structures. Democratic self-interest is treated as dangerous; intelligent minorities must manage the masses "for their own benefit."

This resembles Leninism—doing things "for you" and "in everyone's interest." Historically, this similarity helped ideologists shift between Stalinism and American power support without changing fundamental positions about who should decide.

Historical Origins

This system evolved from World War I, which transformed American global position. Though already the world's wealthiest nation, the U.S. wasn't a major world player before 1914. The war changed everything.

Britain needed American involvement and operated a Ministry of Information, producing fabrications about "Hun atrocities." This propaganda targeted American intellectuals as "most gullible" and best positioned to disseminate messaging through their networks. British documents later released showed they aimed "to control the thought of the entire world," particularly America.

President Woodrow Wilson won election in 1916 opposing war—the country was intensely pacifist. Yet intervention required converting this population into "raving anti-German lunatics." The Committee on Public Information (the "Creel Commission") executed this propaganda transformation remarkably quickly.

Industrial Development

Hitler read these achievements and concluded Germany lost World War I through propaganda failure. He promised superior systems for the next war.

American business elite likewise recognized propaganda's power. Growing democratization—more voters, new immigrants, increased participation—threatened their private-club governance model. The solution: control public thought through the newly invented mass public relations industry, drawing leadership directly from the Creel Commission.

The Bernays Manual

Edward Bernays, emerging from the Creel Commission, authored Propaganda (circa 1925), applying wartime lessons. He described propaganda techniques as enabling "intelligent minorities" to "regiment the public mind every bit as much as an army regiments their bodies." His fame came from cigarette marketing campaigns that normalized women smoking—an example of mass perception manipulation benefiting commercial interests.

His book became the public relations industry's foundational manual.

The Manufacture of Consent

Walter Lippmann, another Creel Commission figure and America's most respected serious journalist for half a century, explicitly discussed applying propaganda lessons to democracy. He termed this new technique the "manufacture of consent"—a phrase later borrowed for the Chomsky and Herman book.

Manufacturing consent overcomes formal voting rights by "structuring choices and attitudes" so populations "will always do what we tell them, even if they have formal ways to participate." This represents "real democracy" working properly.

Academic Foundations

Harold Lasswell founded academic communications and political science, producing propaganda studies. His work explicitly rejected "democratic dogmatism," originating arguments about elite management necessity.

Conservative British political parties reviewed their Ministry of Information successes and concluded that advancing democratization required treating "politics as political warfare"—applying propaganda mechanisms to control public thought.

Hidden Curriculum

These foundational doctrines remain "mainstream literature but only for people on the inside." College education omits classics about thought control and James Madison's constitutional convention statements about protecting "the minority of the opulent against the majority."

Academic scholarship actively obscures these founding principles unless readers search extensively.

Summary

This system operates through institutional structure reinforced by doctrine, producing entirely predictable outcomes. Understanding the mechanisms—how elites discuss control among themselves, how educational filtering works, how media corporations maximize advertiser value—enables accurate predictions about what information reaches whom.

Separately, diversionary content keeps uninformed outsiders engaged with entertainment rather than serious governance questions.
